Transformations of remnants

Turbulent times may also be truly interesting ones too. And my city has gone through, and survived, many of them, its glory came and gone like the constant movement of the sea in the ebb and the high flow.

Destroyed or resused architectural parts, embedded in the houses built after the hastily made reconstructions, or scattered in gardens and empty spaces, try to talk to the passers-by, and to make them ask themselves, which was the moment in the continuous passing of time, that they were brutally braken, burnt or defaced, only to be put sometime later again in another place; their function sometimes being completely different from the previous one.

As a child, I have curiously searched for such reused architectural parts. For instance, a broken capital, possibly Roman or Ottoman, embedded as a decorative figure in the wall of a house, built after the last major destruction of Athens, during the revolution of 1821. Or the famous parts of the columns and the zoophorus of the archaic temple of Acropolis, Ekatompedon, that were used to augment the fortifications of Acropolis, after its first destruction, during the Persian Wars of the early 5th century BCE.
